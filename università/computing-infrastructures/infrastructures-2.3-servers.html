Server

Remember the architectural overview of a warehouse-scale computer.

Servers are the main processing equipment being the basic building blocks of DCs and WSCs. They are of different types and are hosted in individual shelves.
Servers are interconnected by hierarchies of networks and supported by the shared power and cooling infrastructure.

They are like ordinary PCs but with a form factor that allows to fit them into the shelves:

  • Rack
  • Blade enclosure format
  • Tower

Furthermore, servers usually house:

  • Motherboard: component that provides slots to install CPUs, memory modules (DIMMs), local storage (SSDs or HDDs) and network interface cards (NICs).
  • Chipset
  • Additional plug-in components (es: GPUs)
Servers in WSC

Warehouse-scale computers are built using a limited set of standardized hardware components and system software (the operating systems and other fundamental software components managing the infrastructure) across the entire facility

Rack servers

Racks are special shelves that accommodate all the IT equipment and allow their interconnection. That is, rack servers are stored in racks.
Server racks heights are measured in rack units, or "U's": . Width and depth of racks vary across WSCs.

However, racks are not only a physical structure, as they handle shared power infrastructure and provide a rack-level network switch, called TOR (top of rack switch).

Pros

  • Failure containment: easy to identify, remove and replace a malfunctioning server with another
  • Simplified cable management: easy and efficient to organize cables
  • Cost-effective: computing power and efficiency at relatively lower costs.

Cons

  • Power usage: high component density thus needs additional cooling systems
  • Maintenance: Since multiple devices are placed in racks together, maintaining them gets considerably tough with the increasing number of racks

Tower servers

Tower server looks and feels like a traditional tower PC. This provides some pros and cons.

Pros:

  • Scalability and ease of upgrade (components can be upgraded based on necessity)
  • Cost-effective: cheap
  • Cools easily: low component density thus it cools down easily

Cons:

  • Consumes a lot of space
  • Provides basic level of performance: ideal for small businesses that have a limited number of clients.
  • Complicated cable management

Blade servers

They can be called hybrid rack servers, in which servers are placed inside blade enclosures, forming a blade system

Pros

  • Size: they are the smallest and most compact servers. This is their biggest advantage
  • Cabling: almost no need for cabling
  • Centralized management: can connect all the blades through a single interface, making the maintenance and monitoring easy.
  • Load balancing, fail-over, scalability: Uniform system, shared components (including network), simple addition/removal of servers
    Cons
  • Expensive configuration: initial configuration or the setup might require heavy efforts in complex environments.
  • Vendor lock-in: they typically require the use of the manufacturer specific blades and enclosures.
  • Cooling: High component density thus need for special cooling systems.

Accelerators

Complexity doubles every 3.5 months, while according to Moore's law processors performances doubles every 18-24 months, and nowadays even more slowely (every 4 years). Thus, in order to cope with the needing of better performances WSC's deploy special accelerator hardware:

  • GPU
  • TPU
  • FPGA

GPU

A GPU is a specialized processor designed for data-parallel computations, meaning it can execute the same program on many data elements simultaneously. Unlike a CPU, which is optimized for sequential task execution, a GPU has thousands of smaller cores that work together to handle large-scale parallel operations efficiently.

In scientific computing, GPU acceleration is used to map complex computations onto matrix operations, which are fundamental in areas like physics simulations, deep learning, and image processing.

To program GPUs effectively, high-level parallel computing languages like CUDA (by NVIDIA) and OpenCL (open standard) are used. These frameworks allow developers to write code that leverages the GPU’s parallel architecture for massive speedups.

Because of this architecture, GPUs can be up to 1000x faster than CPUs for specific workloads that involve large-scale parallelism, such as deep learning, cryptography, and high-performance computing tasks

TPU (Tensor Processing Unit)

While suited to Machine Learning, GPUSs are still relatively general purpose devices. In recent years, designers further specialized them to ML-specific hardware: TPUs.

TPUs have custom-build integrated circuits developed specifically for machine learning and tailored for ML frameworks as TensorFlow and Pytorch.

A tensor is an n-dimensional matrix and TPU are specialized in computations involving tensors, from building and refining the model (training) to applying it to solve problems with new data (inference). In fact, each tensor core has an array for matrix computations and a connection to high bandwidth memory to store parameters and intermediate values during computations.

  • TPUv1: Simple, inference-only
  • TPUv2 and later: Support both training and inference, more modular and scalable
  • TPUv3–v6: Increasing compute power, memory, and system scale
    → Evolved into supercomputers for AI

From an architectural point of view, each core has:

  • A matrix unit (MXU) for fast math
  • High-bandwidth memory (HBM) for handling large models

Multiple cores work together, connected through fast networks

TPU Pods

TPUs are grouped into pods: clusters of many TPUs working as one. This enables training very large models quickly and efficiently

AWS counterpart

While TPUs are developed by Google, AWS is developing its alternatives

  • Graviton4: General-purpose, efficient CPUs (ARM-based)
  • Trainium2: Custom for training large AI models
  • Inferentia2: Custom for running (inference) AI models efficiently

Field-Programmable Gate Array (FPGA)

FPGAs are programmable HW devices. They have arrays of logic gates that can be freely programmed ("configured") by the user, Furthermore, they involve digital subcircuits (called configurable logic blocks - CLBs) that efficiently implement common functions.

  • HDL: uses text to introduce components and create interconnections.
  • VHDL and Verilog: hardware description languages (HDLs) that allow to describe hardware.

While not a replacement for traditional processors, FPGAs serve as a complementary
technology, offering potential performance and efficiency improvements for specific
data center workloads.

FPGA in datacenters

Using FPGAs inside data centers enables for a lower carbon footprint since they are flexible, in fact it is possible to riconfigurate them instead of re-implement them.

They have multiple applications inside data centers:

  • Network acceleration: FPGAs can offload specific network processing tasks from CPUs, improving overall network performance and reducing CPU workload
  • Security acceleration: Encryption, decryption, and other security-related tasks can be accelerated using FPGAs, enhancing data center security while maintaining performance
  • Data analytics: FPGAs can be used to accelerate specific algorithms used in data analytics workloads, leading to faster data processing and analysis
  • Machine learning: FPGAs can be configured to implement specific machine learning algorithms efficiently, potentially offering performance advantages for specialized tasks

From racks to datacenters

The IT equipment is stored into corridors, and organized into racks.
It is important to notice that server racks are never Back-to-back. Corridors where servers are located are split into:

  • cold aisle: is where front panels of the equipment is reachable
  • warm aisle: where back connections are located

How does the cooling work? Cold air flows from the cool aisle, cools down the equipment, and leave the room from the warm aisle.

Q: [2.3] What are servers in a data center infrastructure?
A: Servers are the primary processing units in data centers and warehouse-scale computers. They come in different form factors—rack, blade, and tower—and house components like CPUs, memory, storage, and network interfaces. They are supported by shared power and cooling systems, and interconnected through network hierarchies.

Q: [2.3] What are rack servers and their pros and cons?
A: Rack servers are servers mounted in standardized shelves (racks) that offer centralized power and networking (via TOR switches). They allow for easy maintenance, efficient cabling, and good cost-performance balance. However, they consume a lot of power, require extra cooling, and become harder to maintain as density increases.

Q: [2.3] What are tower servers and their pros and cons?
A: Tower servers resemble traditional desktop PCs and are used for smaller-scale deployments. They are inexpensive, easy to upgrade, and simple to cool. However, they take up more physical space, offer lower performance, and make cable management difficult.

Q: [2.3] What are blade servers and their pros and cons?
A: Blade servers are compact servers placed in a shared blade enclosure. They offer minimal cabling, centralized management, and scalable performance through load balancing and shared components. Their disadvantages include high setup cost, dependence on specific vendors, and the need for enhanced cooling due to high density.

Q: [2.3] What are accelerators and why are they used in data centers?
A: Accelerators (GPU, TPU, FPGA) are specialized hardware used to overcome the slow pace of general-purpose processor improvements. They enhance performance and efficiency for specific workloads like machine learning, simulations, and encryption.

Q: [2.3] What is a GPU and what is it used for in data centers?
A: A GPU is a parallel processor designed for data-parallel workloads. It uses thousands of small cores to perform simultaneous operations, especially useful in deep learning, simulations, and high-performance computing. GPUs are programmed using CUDA or OpenCL for parallel computation.

Q: [2.3] What are TPUs and how do they work?
A: TPUs (Tensor Processing Units) are Google's custom hardware for machine learning tasks like training and inference. Each TPU core includes a matrix computation unit (MXU) and high-bandwidth memory (HBM). TPUs are specialized for tensor operations and are grouped into pods for scalable training. They have evolved from simple inference accelerators (v1) to supercomputing-class systems (v6).

Q: [2.3] What are TPU Pods and why are they important?
A: TPU Pods are clusters of TPUs interconnected to function as a unified system. They enable efficient large-scale training by combining high compute power and fast internal communication, supporting modern ML models like LLMs.

Q: [2.3] What are AWS’s hardware accelerators for AI?
A: AWS offers Graviton4 (ARM-based CPUs for general tasks), Trainium2 (optimized for training large AI models), and Inferentia2 (optimized for inference). These serve as alternatives to Google's TPUs, designed to lower cost and improve efficiency in AI workloads.

Q: [2.3] What are FPGAs and how are they used in data centers?
A: FPGAs are programmable hardware that can be reconfigured for specific tasks. In data centers, they are used for network acceleration, security, data analytics, and machine learning. Their flexibility reduces the need for new hardware and supports energy-efficient operation.

Q: [2.3] How is cooling managed in data centers?
A: Servers are organized in racks within corridors. Cooling is achieved through hot and cold aisle separation: cold air enters through the cold aisle, flows through equipment, and exits through the warm aisle. Racks are never placed back-to-back to maintain airflow efficiency.
