Abstraction traps

Introduction

  • Desirable properties of a system can be described in terms of inputs and outputs. Internals and the context are abstracted away
  • Performance metrics easily computed by input and output
  • Often developers want to achieve fairness by implementing it as a metrics to optimize.
  • Authors argument: by abstracting away the context that surrounds the system, information necessary to achieve fairness is missed, even understand fairness as concept can be difficult.
  • That is, treat fairness and justice as something that can have a technology application separate from the social context is a mistake: abstraction error
  • Authors present five failures models that describe how one can fail to consider interactions between technical systems and social context

Framing trap

Definition

Failure to model the entire system over which a social criterion, such as fairness, will be enforced

  • Algorithmic frame is the frame that includes only the algorithm which takes input data and produces output data.
  • Efficacy of the algorithmic frame is evaluated by input and output data, this is the end to end property
  • Algorithmic frame abstracts away everything else. Thus is impossible to achieve fairness.
  • Better to put input and output data into the frame: data frame.
  • Examining inputs and outputs explicitly allows researchers to evaluate how data representations and labeling decisions shape the fairness of ML models. By taking into account those examinations it is possible to better approximate fairness.
  • However, data frame doesn't remove the problems of the data frame approach, since it only involves further considerations of the inputs and the outputs but the core of the model is still to formalize fairness mathematically (by means of fairness metrics and constraint)
  • Sociotechnical frame is a better option, because it moves the social context (decisions by humans and human institutions) within the abstraction boundary, it allows to analyze fairness as an end-to-end property.
  • Example of risk assessment: a frame that does not incorporate a model of the judge's decisions cannot provide the end-to-end guarantees that this frame requires

The portability trap

Definition

Failure to understand how re-purposing algorithmic solutions designed for one social context may be misleading, inaccurate, or otherwise do harm when applied to a different context.

  • Portability is a core feature of computer science tools. Since problem are characterized by the task to be solved, the task is important, not the context of applicability. Designers are so taught that portability is the ultimate goal of a system design.
  • Machine learning tools are designed to achieve portability
  • Portability is still a core value of the fair-ML research too. In fact, for the sake of formalism, assumptions are made on the world in order for the ML tool to work.
  • The problem: certain assumption won't hold in every context. In fact even in the same domain (but in different geographical contexts) assumptions can be different.
  • ML-tools should be designed by considering the assumption that hold in the specific domain of application. But this means making a system which is non portable between different context.

The formalism trap

Definition

Failure to account for the full meaning of social concepts such as fairness, which can be procedural, contextual, and contestable, and cannot be resolved through mathematical formalisms

  • In order to incorporate mathematical definition of fairness into machine learning systems, fairness should be defined.
  • Difficult to define fairness, especially in mathematical terms
  • Problem 1: there can be conflicting definitions of fairness that depend on the relevant social context. Once fairness is defined within a specific social context (e.g., equal opportunity, demographic parity), it can be formalized mathematically using constraints or optimization, however the problem raises when we have multiple fairness definitions together, which are difficult to represent in mathematical terms if they are conflicting in some way (es: false negatives and false positives have very different meaning in different social context)
  • Problem 2: fairness cannot be defined at all, since different definition are procedural, contextual or contestable:
    • procedurality: fair-ML definitions are outcome based unlike law which is procedural. That is there cannot be distinction between firing someone for racial causes or firing someone otherwise, for ML the outcome is the same.
    • contextuality: ML cannot distinguish by itself to good and wrong discrimination since those depend on the context and only be comprehend with access to cultural knowledge.
    • contestability: Discrimination and fairness are political contestable and shifting. Liberal society depends on the idea that some concepts will be fundamentally contestable and will shift over time, that communities should be allowed to collectively define norms and laws. To set them in stone or in code is to pick sides, and to do so without transparent process violates democratic ideals.

The ripple effect trap

Definition

Failure to understand how the insertion of technology into an existing social system changes the behaviors and embedded values of the pre-existing system

  • Inserting technology in a social context can have intended and unintended consequences.
  • Important unintended consequence is how people will respond to the intervention.
  • Without knowing how a technology will interact with a pre-existing social system it is not possible to understand if its introduction will improve fairness
  • Example: how will the judge who see numeric risk assessment recommendation react to it?
  • Technologies can also alter the underlying social values and incentives embedded in the social system. It can be that quantifiable metrics (for which ML system give a score) will become unconsciously privileged over other values that should have the same importance. (es: education vs incapacitation)
  • Attention is needed to understand when technologies trigger value shifts in social systems

The solutionism trap

Definition

Failure to recognize the possibility that the best solution to a problem may not involve technology

  • Technological solutionism as the idea of there is no concept of the system without technical intervention: "if you have a hammer, everything looks like a nail"
  • Starting from the technology and trying to improve it in order to make it as efficient as possible for solving a certain problem does not give you the opportunity to evaluate whether the technology should be built in the first place
  • Two case in which this approach is wrong:
    • Where fairness definitions are contested or shifts, so it should be avoided to calcify them in code (see The formalism trap)
    • When modeling required would be so complex as to be computationally intractable (es: trying to model political preference)
  • Furthermore the problem is that if there is not enough information to understand everything that is important to a context, approximations are as likely to make things worse. Especially when the stakes are high (es: risk assessment), it is prudent to study what might happen before implementing a technology simply based on a potential improvement.

Sociotechnical perspective

Introduction

  • Impossible to achieve fairness in ML systems without a sociotechnical view. That is a strong framework that can help us enable process and order in messy reality.
  • Principle of sociotechnical system (STS): both social and technical perspectives must be considered in a design process.
  • A sociotechnical view helps us to see the influence that social actors have on the technology development and determine the efficacy of that technology. To see the connection between a technology and the context in which it is applied can help us to build differently.
  • Abstraction traps can be avoided by means of a sociotechnical view

STS lens on Framing trap

  • Heterogeneus engineering: consider both human activities and machine ones at the same time. What technical parts do and how human (which are involved directly or indirectly) contribute.
  • Boundaries of abstractions include people and social systems
  • Since social context is now part of the system, modelling social values like fairness is easier and can be done by considering them properties of the entire sociotechnical system and not only an algorithmic metrics.
  • Example of cellphone: developing a cellphone without considering the social and technical context (network, regulators entities ecc...) is not useful, the same thing happens if we develop fair-ML systems separating it from the social context.
  • Of course this approach eliminates benefits of abstraction and so elevate complexity, but handling complexity is necessary to guarantee that the technology will operate as intended.

STS lens on Portability

  • Technology designers hope to embed a "script" that dictate how technologies are supposed to be used. In case of fair-ML, script is producing fair outcomes in social contexts
  • However, those scripts only work if all the social and technical elements of a technology are assembled properly.
  • Thus, outcomes of the use of a technology are disrupted as soon as the technology is moved in a different context.
  • Portable orientated technologies cannot achieve certain goals. For example as fairness is not tied to specific piece of technologies but to specific social context, it is not effective to attach a "fair" script to a ML system as that script will not be true as soon as the system is moved in other contexts.
  • Sociotechnical view (es: applying frame) make system not portable but it is necessary to achieve certain objectives.

STS lens on Formalism trap

  • Different definitions of fairness solve different groups problems and thus address different contextual concerns.

  • Social Construction of Technology program (SCOT) explains how the development of technologies works:

    • Interpretive flexibility: phase in which a technology is first introduced and different relevant social groups try to shape its development by asking developers to make it address their problems. This way different versions of the product are developed, until a closure is reached.
    • Closure: the point at which a technology stabilizes because one or more relevant social groups perceive the problem it addresses as solved. Closure occurs when there is no longer significant pressure to modify the technology, reducing interpretive flexibility. This is the result of the influence of the various relevant groups.
    • Of course the more powerful groups tend to have better chances to shape the technology as they need. Not necessarily more powerful groups but also the groups which have major influence on designers, who are always kingmakers but also a social group themselves.
    • Closure is thus not always reached when the best solution is found but is a product of other social mechanisms:
      • rhetorical closure: relevant groups describe the problem as solved, and move on.
      • closure by redefinition of the problem: relevant groups redefine the problem such that the solution they already have at hand, becomes the solution to the problem.

  • In the context of fair-ML, there is agreement that this technology is needed, but different social groups ask for a different version of the model, which address different definitions of fairness.

  • Recognizing that a certain version of "fairness" is only one interpretation of the problem is critical for considering potential solutions that may address the needs of other relevant social groups.

  • The assumption "fairness can be defined mathematically" may become a general agreement and lead designers to assume that a system is fair when it has a mathematical model of fairness built into.

  • SCOT allows to understand the mechanisms that shape technological development. Also illustrates that problem of formalism are related to which problem should be resolved, by who and who has influence over the process.

STS lens on Ripple Effect Trap

  • Difficult to predict change a technology will cause, but common ripple effects are well documented in literature.
  • Knowing common "ripple effects" in advance can help. Technology can produce:
    • Reinforcement politics: existing groups use new technological system to reinforce their power or position or on the contrary to have an opportunity to argue for more power
    • Reactivity behaviors: altering the social context that the original design was meant to support (es: questionnaires on criminal personality that cause dishonest answers, while the context was assumed to provide honest answers). This can lead to destabilize existing values, incentives, and structures to such a degree that the designed tool no longer solves the critical problems in the domain.
    • Arise of new social groups: once a technology is part of the social context, new relevant social groups can arise and radically reinterpret it, return it to a state of interpretive flexibility, and suggest new mechanisms for closure. (es: new government that change the use of risk assessment tools).
  • Unintended consequences cannot be completely eliminate but we can control some ripple effects and troubles in advance.

STS lens on solutionism trap

  • Computer science programs do not incentivize the social science research necessary to ensure robust system use in the world
  • However, considering the complex sociotechnical system at play it is possible to reach the conclusion that the best way is to shelve technological fix, or to build the system on different assumptions or approaches.
  • not all problems can or should be solved with technology. In such cases, taking ourselves out of the equation as a relevant social group requires and rewards researcher humility.

Takeaways: what fair-ML researchers can and should do

Key Points

Abstraction Traps

Introduzione

  • Abstraction error: trattare la giustizia come separata dal contesto sociale è un errore.
  • Modelli ML di equità astraggono il contesto sociale → Perdita di informazioni necessarie per comprendere ed implementare equità.
  • Cinque trappole: descrivono come ignorare le interazioni tra sistemi tecnici e contesto sociale.

1. Framing Trap

  • Definizione: Non modellare l’intero sistema su cui si applica un criterio sociale (es: equità).
  • Algoritmic frame: considera solo input/output dell’algoritmo → Non permette equità.
  • Data frame: analizza esplicitamente input/output per migliorare approssimazione dell'equità, ma non elimina la formalizzazione.
  • Sociotechnical frame: include il contesto sociale dentro i confini dell’astrazione → Permette analisi di equità end-to-end.
    • Es: strumenti di valutazione del rischio che includono decisioni dei giudici.

2. Portability Trap

  • Definizione: Errore nel riproporre soluzioni algoritmiche in contesti sociali diversi senza adattamenti.
  • Problema: Assunzioni valide in un contesto possono fallire in altri → Soluzioni ML non portabili tra contesti.
  • Valore sociotecnico: Progettare sistemi considerando il dominio specifico, sacrificando portabilità.

3. Formalism Trap

  • Definizione: Non considerare che concetti sociali (es: equità) non possono essere ridotti solo a formalismi matematici.
  • Problemi:
    • Definizioni conflittuali: es. parità demografica vs opportunità uguali.
    • Proceduralità: ML basa l’equità sui risultati, ma la legge considera le intenzioni (es: discriminazione).
    • Contestualità: La discriminazione dipende dal contesto culturale.
    • Contestabilità: Concetti come equità sono politici, mutano nel tempo e necessitano processi democratici.

4. Ripple Effect Trap

  • Definizione: Non considerare come l’introduzione della tecnologia modifica valori e comportamenti del sistema sociale preesistente.
  • Effetti comuni:
    • Reinforcement politics: rafforzare poteri esistenti o permettere nuovi equilibri di potere.
    • Reactivity behaviors: modificare incentivi e strutture sociali in modo da rendere inefficace il design originale.
    • Nuovi gruppi sociali: tecnologie possono essere reinterpretate e riportate in stato di flessibilità interpretativa.

5. Solutionism Trap

  • Definizione: Supporre che la tecnologia sia sempre la soluzione migliore.
  • Problemi:
    • Concetti contestati: Evitare di "fissare" definizioni mutevoli (es: equità) nel codice.
    • Modellazione complessa: Alcuni sistemi sono troppo complessi da modellare accuratamente.
    • Alta posta in gioco: Approssimazioni possono peggiorare la situazione (es: valutazioni del rischio).
  • Approccio sociotecnico: Riflettere se la tecnologia sia la soluzione migliore o se il problema necessiti altri strumenti.

Prospettiva Sociotecnica (STS)

Principi fondamentali

  • Design deve considerare sia prospettive sociali che tecniche.
  • Evitare trappole richiede integrazione del contesto sociale nel processo di progettazione.

STS su Framing Trap

  • Heterogeneous engineering: integrare attività umane e tecniche.
  • Modelli sociotecnici includono valori sociali (es: equità) come proprietà del sistema intero.

STS su Portability Trap

  • Script tecnologico: comportamenti desiderati legati alla tecnologia → Fragile in contesti diversi.
  • Equità è legata al contesto sociale → Necessaria progettazione contestualizzata.

STS su Formalism Trap

  • SCOT: sviluppo tecnologico influenzato da gruppi sociali.
    • Flessibilità interpretativa: Gruppi sociali modellano tecnologia → Diverse versioni.
    • Chiusura: Stabilizzazione quando gruppi percepiscono problema risolto (es: influenza di gruppi più potenti).
  • Diversi gruppi definiscono equità diversamente → Necessità di considerare altre prospettive.

STS su Ripple Effect Trap

  • Conoscere effetti comuni in anticipo aiuta:
    • Reinforcement politics e Reactivity behaviors.
    • Nuovi gruppi sociali possono riaprire flessibilità interpretativa.
  • Controllare alcune conseguenze non previste è possibile.

STS su Solutionism Trap

  • Ricerca sociotecnica necessaria per progettazione robusta.
  • Alcuni problemi non richiedono soluzioni tecnologiche.
  • Approccio umile: a volte è meglio non intervenire tecnologicamente.

Takeaways

  • Necessario integrare prospettiva sociotecnica per evitare trappole.
  • Equità richiede visione complessiva, non solo formale.
  • Riflettere su obiettivi, assunzioni e conseguenze tecnologiche.
