Chapter summary

Morality and Ethics

While morality is the totality of opinions about what is good and right, ethics is the critical reflection on morality. Normative ethics not just describes what morality is but it judges morality and tries to formulate answers to questions like: What kind of person should I be? and How should I act? Normative ethics, therefore, tries to come to certain normative judgments.

Characteristics of Normative Ethics

However, it is not a manual or an unambiguous code in which you can look up the answer to how to act in a difficult situation. Rather, it is an area characterized by a variety of partly conflicting ethical theories about how to act.

Three Main Ethical Theories

The three best-known ethical theories in Western philosophy are:

  • Consequentialism: Focuses on the consequences of actions.
  • Deontology: Focuses on the actions themselves.
  • Virtue ethics: Focuses on the acting person and their character traits.

Consequentialism and Utilitarianism

Utilitarianism is a main variety of consequentialism. It measures consequences by their effect on one value: pleasure or human happiness. It is based on the so-called utility principle: The greatest happiness for the greatest number. Utilitarianism requires drawing up a moral balance sheet or a cost-benefit analysis to determine what action has the best consequences.

Criticisms of Utilitarianism

Despite its intuitive attractiveness, utilitarianism has been heavily criticized:

  1. Exploitation: To address this, John Stuart Mill formulated the freedom principle: Everyone is free to strive for their own pleasure, as long as they do not deny or hinder the pleasure of others.
  2. Intrinsic Rightness or Wrongness: Actions like lying are sometimes right or wrong regardless of consequences. Rule utilitarianism addresses this by focusing on the utility of rules rather than individual acts.
  3. Other Criticisms:
    • Happiness is difficult to measure.
    • Consequences are hard to predict.
    • It ignores the distribution of pleasures and pains.
    • It neglects personal relationships.

Deontology and Kant

Immanuel Kant is the main representative of deontology. He formulated a principle for judging the rightness of actions independent of consequences: the universality principle:

Act only on that maxim which you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law.

Criticisms of Kantian Ethics

Two main criticisms of Kantâs theory are:

  1. It ignores conflicts between norms.
  2. It is too rigid.

A response to these criticisms is to conceive of norms as prima facie norms rather than universal norms applying to all situations.

Virtue Ethics

Virtue ethics focuses on the character of the acting person rather than their actions or consequences. Rooted in Aristotle, it remains relevant today, particularly for engineers. Relevant virtues for engineers include:

  • Professionalism
  • Objectivity
  • Open-mindedness to criticism
  • Stamina
  • Creativity
  • Attention to detail

Criticisms of Virtue Ethics

Criticisms include:

  1. It does not explicitly tell you how to act.
    • Virtue ethicists argue that someone with the right virtues will act differently from someone without them.
  2. Virtues are not unconditionally good.
    • Virtue ethics emphasizes practical wisdom: the ability to make ethical judgments in complex situations.

Care Ethics

A fourth ethical theory is care ethics, which does not focus on abstract principles but rather on relationships between people. This is relevant to engineers, who often work on complex projects with many stakeholders relying on them. One way to apply care ethics is to identify norms that social arrangements in engineering should meet to express due care for all stakeholders.

Conclusion

The diversity of ethical theories shows that there is no single answer to what is right or wrong. However, this does not mean that anything goes. Some actions are morally good or bad according to all theories.

When ethical theories disagree, they still help:

  • Distinguish ethical questions in a concrete situation.
  • Precisely analyze situations.
  • Suggest reasons and arguments for different actions.

Even if ethical theories do not guarantee the right decision, ignoring them and the underlying ethical concerns usually results in plainly unethical decisions.
