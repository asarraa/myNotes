Introduction

Q: What are the characteristics of a dependable system? Explain them
A: the following

  • Availability: system should be ready for use as soon as we need it
  • Reliability: the system should be able to run continuously for long time
  • Safety: it should cause nothing bad to happen
  • Maintainability: it should be easy to fix

Q: What are fail, fault and error? Make an example
A: the following

  • A system fails when it is not able provide its services (es: crash)
  • A failure is the result of an error (es: nullpointerexeption)
  • An error is caused by a fault (es: the programmer didn't check the variable was initialized)

Q: Can all faults be avoided?
A: No

Q: When a system is said to be fault tolerant?
A: A system is said to be fault tolerant if it can provide its services even in presence of faults

Q: How can faults be classified according to their frequency? What are the worse to deal with?
A: in the following ways

  • Transient: occur once an then disappear
  • Intermittent: appear and vanish with no apparent reason
  • Permanent: continue to exist until the failed components are repaired
    Intermittent faults are the worst to deal with.

Q: What are the types of failures in processes and communication channels? What is the preferable one? why?
A: the following

  • Omission failures:
    • Processes: : fail-safe (sometimes wrong, but easily detectable output), fail-stop (detectable crash), fail-silent (undetectable crash)
    • Channels: omissions in delivering the message
  • Byzantine (or arbitrary) failures:
    • Processes: may omit intended processing steps or add more
    • Channels: message content may be corrupted, non-existent messages may be delivered, or real messages may be delivered more than once
  • Timing failures: occur when one of the time limits defined for the system is violated (in synchronous systems only)
    We like omission failures and not byzantine failures, and we can transform byzantine failures into omission failures, using checksum.

Q: What is the key technique to mask a failure?
A: Redundancy (information, time and physical)

Client/server communication

Q: Are crash failures (es: loss of a connection) masked by the TCP protocol? If not, how can mask them with our high-level communication facilities?
A: No, TCP protocol only masks omission failures using acks and re-transmissions but it isn't able to mask crash failures. It's actually impossible to provide complete masking of crash failures (es: print server).

Q: How is a computation (RPC) started by a dead client called? How can a server get rid of it?
A: It's called orphan. There are different approaches:

  • Extermination: RPC’s are logged by the client and orphans killed (on client request) after a reboot -> costly (logging each call) and problem with grand orphans and network partitions.
  • Reincarnation: when a clients reboots it starts a new epoch and sends a broadcast message to servers, who kill old computations started on behalf of that client (including grand orphans). Replies sent by orphans bring an obsolete epoch and can be easily discarded.
  • Gentle reincarnation: as before but servers kill old computations only if owner cannot be located: so if the epoque changed, I ping the client who requested my a computation to see if it is still on
  • Expiration: Remote computation expire after some time. Clients wait to reboot to let remote computations to expire

Process against process failures

Q: Which are the two possible organization of processes in process resilience?
A: the following:

  • Flat group
  • Hierarchical group

Q: In process resilience groups, how large should a group be in case of omission failure and in case of byzantine failure?
A: In case of omission failures (process fail silently), k+1 process allow the system to be k-fault-tolerant.
In case of Byzantine failures, 2k+1 process are required to achieve a k-fault tolerance (a working voting mechanism is needed).

Q: In consensus problem with silent-fails (omission failures), what proprieties must hold?
A: the following

  • Agreement: No two processes decide on different values
  • Validity: if all processes start with the same value v, then v is the only possible decision value
  • Termination: All non-faulty processes eventually decide

Q: Is consensus with unreliable channels achievable?
A: (to be confirmed) If the channels are unreliable, it means messages can be lost, delayed indefinitely, or delivered out of order. In this case, achieving consensus with failed processes becomes extremely difficult, if not impossible, because. Unreliable communication introduces uncertainty. A process might never know if a message was truly lost or if the sender failed. This creates ambiguity, as processes cannot be sure whether a failed process is genuinely down or simply unreachable due to network issues.

A: the following

  • assumptions:

    • Synchronous communication
  • Goal: Reach a consensus in a system with at most f failures and f+1 rounds (steps).

Q: In consensus problem with Byzantine-fails, what proprieties must hold? What are the differences with the consensus in case of silent fails?
A: the following

  • Agreement: No two non-faulty processes decide on different values
  • Validity: if all non-faulty processes start with the same value v, then v is the only possible decision value
  • Termination: All non-faulty processes eventually decide
    The idea is that we cannot guarantee anything about faulty processes (they can be traitors)
    The differences are evidenced in bold

Q: What is the Byzantine generals problem? Describe it and the algorithm to solve it.
A: The Byzantine generals problem is the consensus in case of byzantine failures (reliable communication + synchronicity but process can have byzantine failures), formulated in terms of armies and general by Lamport.
In this problem we have n generals in the hills who have to reach a coordinated decision on whether to attack or retreat. They announce their troop strengths and decide based on the total number of troops. However, some generals are traitors.

Reliable group communication

Q: What is reliable group communication?
A: Reliable group communication refers to the ability of a system to ensure that messages sent to a group of processes are delivered reliably, even in the presence of certain failures. This is important for systems that rely on process replication to achieve resilience, as it ensures that all replicas maintain consistency.

Q: How to achieve reliable group communication when groups are fixed and process are non-faulty, on top of unreliable multicast?
A: with the following approaches

  • Basic approach: every receiver replies with an ACK to acknowledge that the message has been received. This means a lot of acks, which will lead to ack implosion. Alternatively, a receiver replies only if something goes bad (es: stops receiving packets). Notice that in order to do this, you need to understand that you missed a packet. This can be done for example by numbering packets. This reduces the number of messages, but it requires the sender to cache messages in case they need to re-transmit them upon receiving a NACK.
  • Non-hierarchical feedback control: Hierarchical Feedback Control organizes receivers into groups, each with a coordinator. These coordinators form a tree structure with the sender at the root. When a receiver detects a missing message, it sends a negative acknowledgment (NACK) to its coordinator rather than directly to the sender. The coordinator consolidates these NACKs before passing them up the tree, reducing the number of duplicate NACKs and improving scalability. This structure helps manage large groups more efficiently but requires processing at each level of the hierarchy.

Q: What is atomic multicast problem in reliable process communication?
A: The atomic multicast problem is the need for a message to be delivered either to all the members of a group or to none, and that the order of messages should be the same at all receivers. These requirements are needed for guarantee reliable communication in case of faulty processes.

Q: What is close synchrony? Can it be achieved in presence of failures?
A: It is the kind of synchrony requiring that:

  • Multicast messages and group membership changes are seen in the same order by processes in the group
  • the sending of a message is indivisible by its receiving: no faults can occur in meanwhile, so a multicast to a process group has to be delivered to its full membership
    No, it can be achieved i presence of failures.

Q: What is Virtual synchrony?
A: Virtual synchrony is a weaker model that compensates for failures. In this model, the following rules apply:

  • Crashed processes are purged from the group and have to join again
  • Messages from a correct process are processed by all correct processes
  • Messages from a failing process are processed either by all correct members or by none
  • Only relevant messages are received in a specific order
  • Must be guaranteed that messages are always delivered before or after a view change by all the processes.

Q: In virtual synchrony, what is view change?
A: is a special multicast message that informs the components of a group that there was a change in the group membership (es: some process crashed or some rejoined)

Q: What is a group view in the virtual synchrony context?
A: In the virtual synchrony context, Every message is associated with a "group view," which is the set of processes that are part of the group at the time the message was sent.

Q: In which component virtual synchrony takes place?
A: Communication layer is in charge to receive information about crashes and messages and reorder them in order to never let application receive situations that are against virtual synchrony principles.

Q: What is the total ordering requirement?
A: Messages must be delivered to every group member in the same order (whatever kid of ordering is chosen)

Q: What are the possible kind of ordering in the virtual synchrony property?
A: the following

  • Unordered multicast
  • FIFO-ordered multicast: Messages from the same sender are received in the same order by all receivers
  • Causally-ordered multicast: (es: Lamport order)

Q: How was virtual synchrony implemented in the ISIS project? On what kind of assumption the protocol relies on?
A: In the Isis implementation of virtual synchrony, multiple non-shared TCP channels are utilized for communication among processes. When a process detects that another process has crashed, it sends a view message to inform other processes of the failure.
Upon receiving this message, the other processes respond by multicasting the set of unstable messages—messages that some processes have received but have not yet been acknowledged by others. This mechanism ensures that all remaining processes can access any messages that the crashed process had successfully sent to at least one other process.
Once this communication is established, the application can be informed about the crash, and the new group view can be installed, reflecting the updated state of the system.
This implementation relies on the assumption that no additional processes will crash during this period of communication, maintaining system integrity.
Overall, virtual synchrony provides a robust solution for managing process failures, ensuring reliable communication and consistent state across distributed systems.

Recovery techniques

Q: What are forward recovery and backward recovery?
A: They are techniques to take back to a correct state process which had failed.

  • Backward recovery: the system is brought back to a previously saved correct state.
  • Forward recovery: the system is brought into a new correct state from which execution can be resumed.

Q: What is independent check-pointing? What problems does it have?
A: In independent check-pointing, each process independently records its own state. The problem is the domino effect (If one process fails and needs to rollback to a previous checkpoint, it might force other processes to also rollback to their own previous checkpoints, potentially causing a cascade of rollbacks throughout the system.), also it is not trivial to implement since each message should record a reference to the checkpoint interval from which it was sent.

Q: Which techniques can be used to compute the recovery line when using check-pointing?
A: the following

  • Rollback dependency graph: A dependency between intervals becomes a dependency between the ending checkpoint of the sender's interval and the ending checkpoint of the receiver's interval. The recovery line is computed by marking the nodes corresponding to the failure states and then marking all those which are reachable from one of them. These marked nodes are no good (and are then marked). Each process rolls back to the last unmarked checkpoint.
  • Checkpoint graph: Here the dependency between interval becomes a dependency between the starting checkpoint of the server's interval and the ending checkpoint of the receiver's interval. For computing the recovery line here you discard the last checkpoint of the failed process and start taking the last checkpoint of each process as part of the recovery line. This is the first hypothesis, from here you have check if there's a dependency (a path) between two of them (also indirect path, also including HORIZONTAL LINES!!), if so, you discard the checkpoint at the arriving point of the dependency and take the checkpoint before it for that process, making a new hypothesis and starting the check again.

Q: What are some solution to the complexity of the indipendet checkpointing technique?
A: The solution is to coordinate checkpoints.

  • Simple solution:
    1. a coordinator sends CHKP-REQ (checkpoint request).
    2. Receivers take checkpoint and queue other outgoing messages (delivered by applications)
    3. When done send ACK to coordinator
    4. When all done coordinator sends CHKP-DONE
  • Simple solution with improvement - Incremental snapshot: - When using the incremental snapshot approach, only those processes that have received messages from a specific process (like the coordinator) after its last checkpoint will be required to take a new checkpoint. This is because these processes are the ones whose states are affected by the actions of the coordinator, ensuring that the system can recover properly without unnecessary overhead.
  • [Global snapshot](synchronization link)
  • Communication-induced checkpointing: Processes take checkpoints independently but piggyback information on messages to allow the other processes to determine whether they should also take a checkpoint

Q: In Logging, when a message is said stable?
A: When it an no longer be lost, that means it has been written to stable storage.

Q: In Logging, how can an orphan process be characterized using the DEP(m) and COPY(m) groups?
A: For each unstable message m we define

  • DEP(m): processes that depend on the delivery of m, i. e those to which m has been delivered or to which a message m’ dependent on m has been delivered.
  • COPY(m): Processes that have a copy of m, but not yet in stable storage. They are those processes that hand over a copy of m that could be used to replay it.
    We can then characterize an orphan process in the following way:
    Let Q be one of the surviving processes after one or more crashes. Q is orphan if there exists m such that Q is in DEP(m) and all processes in COPY(m) have crashe. This means that there is no way to replay m since it has been lost.

Q: How can avoid orphan processes?
A: When a process become dependent on a copy of m, it keeps m (makes it stable). So, it is both DEP(m) and COPY(m), so it can't be orphan since it has a copy of the message.

Q: What is pessimistic logging?
A: Pessimistic logging protocols ensure that no orphan processes are ever created. In fact it prescrives that any unstable message m is delivered to at most one process, which can't send any other message until it writes m to stable storage.

Q: What is optimistic logging?
A: Optimistic logging protocols allow orphans to be created and force them to roll back until they are no longer orphans. In fact, Messages are logged asynchronously, with the assumption that they will be logged before any faults occur; then if all processes in COPY(m) crash, then all processes in DEP(m) are rolled back to a state where they are no longer in DEP(m).
