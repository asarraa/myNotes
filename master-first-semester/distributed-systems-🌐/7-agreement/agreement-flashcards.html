Consensus Overview

Q: What is the primary goal of consensus in distributed systems?
A: The goal is for a majority of nodes to agree on a single value or decision, ensuring consistency across the system.

Atomic Commit Protocols

Q: What is the purpose of Atomic Commit in distributed databases?
A: To ensure that all participating nodes in a transaction either commit or abort the transaction as a whole.

Q: What are the conditions for committing a transaction in an Atomic Commit protocol?
A: All nodes must vote to commit; if even one node votes to abort, the transaction is aborted.

Two-Phase Commit (2PC)

Q: What are the two phases in the Two-Phase Commit (2PC) protocol?
A: The Prepare phase and the Commit/Abort phase.

Q: When do the coordinator send the prepare message in 2PC?
A: In the Init phase, the coordinator asks all participants if they are ready to commit by sending a "prepare" request. Participants reply with either "yes" (ready to commit) or "no" (abort).

Q: What happens in the Commit phase of 2PC?
A: If all participants vote "yes," the coordinator sends a commit request. If any participant votes "no," the coordinator sends an abort request.

Q: How does 2PC handle participant failure?
A: If a participant fails before replying, the coordinator assumes a timeout and aborts the transaction.

Q: What problem arises in 2PC if the coordinator fails during the Ready state?
A: Participants in the Ready state may block, as they can't make a decision without knowing the global outcome (committed or aborted).

Three-Phase Commit (3PC)

Q: What additional phase does the Three-Phase Commit (3PC) protocol introduce to prevent blocking?
A: The Pre-commit phase, which acts as an intermediary between the Prepare and Commit phases.

Q: How does 3PC handle coordinator failure?
A: If a participant can contact a majority of nodes and one is in the Pre-commit state, it can safely commit. If no one is in Pre-commit, it aborts.

Q: In what type of system does 3PC ensure safety and non-blocking guarantees?
A: In synchronous systems (where nodes can use timeouts to detect failures).

Q: Why is 3PC not widely used in practice despite being non-blocking?
A: It is more complex and expensive than 2PC

Replicated State Machines

Q: What is a replicated state machine?
A: A fault-tolerant service design that replicates state across multiple servers, ensuring they behave consistently by agreeing on the order of operations.

Q: What are the key guarantees of replicated state machines?
A: Safety: All non-failing servers execute the same commands in the same order.
Liveness: The system progresses as long as a majority of servers are functioning and can communicate.

Q: What role does a replicated log play in a replicated state machine?
A: It orders operations across replicas, ensuring all servers apply operations in the same sequence.

Raft Algorithm

Q: What are the three states a node can be in within the Raft consensus algorithm?
A: Follower, Candidate, or Leader.

Q: What are the steps in Raft's normal operation when a client sends a command?
A:

  1. Client sends command to leader.
  2. Leader appends command to its log (uncommitted).
  3. Leader sends AppendEntries to followers.
  4. Once majority confirmation is received, the log entry is committed.
  5. Leader executes the command and responds to the client.
  6. Leader informs followers of committed entries.
  7. Followers execute committed commands in their state machines.

Q: How does Raft ensure fault tolerance when committing log entries?
A: The leader waits for confirmation from a majority of followers before marking the entry as committed.

Raft - Leader Election

Q: How does Raft handle leader election?
A: Followers start an election by becoming candidates if they do not receive heartbeat messages from the leader within a timeout period. Candidates request votes from other nodes, and the one with a majority becomes the new leader.

Q: How does Raft prevent multiple nodes from becoming candidates simultaneously during leader election?
A: Each node has a randomized timeout period before starting an election, reducing the likelihood of simultaneous elections.

Q: What is a term in Raft, and what happens when there is a split vote during an election?
A: A term is a logical time period that includes an election and, if successful, a normal operation phase. In the case of a split vote (no majority), a new term starts with another election.

Paxos

Q: Why is Paxos considered difficult to implement despite being the reference algorithm for consensus?
A: Paxos is complex and hard to understand, which has led to its replacement by simpler algorithms like Raft in practice.

Consensus Failure Models

Q: What assumptions does the replicated state machine model rely on regarding failures?
A:

  1. Communication is unreliable and asynchronous.
  2. Processes may fail and restart, but must remember their state upon recovery.
    A note about pt.1:
    "Replicated state machines, specifically the RAFT consensus algorithm, can function in asynchronous systems and tolerate unreliable communication channels. This means that, in theory, RAFT cannot guarantee both liveness and safety simultaneously due to the FLP (Fischer, Lynch, and Paterson) impossibility theorem. For instance, infinite election terms could occur without a leader being elected if messages are delayed indefinitely. However, in practice, RAFT achieves both properties with high reliability by using probabilistic timeouts, which help ensure that a leader is elected and the system progresses in most cases."

Consensus Guarantees

Q: What is the safety guarantee in consensus algorithms?
A: the safety guarantee ensures that all non-faulty nodes agree on the same sequence of decisions (or values), and once a value is decided, it will never change

Q: What is the liveness guarantee in consensus algorithms?
A: The liveness guarantee in consensus algorithms ensures that the system will eventually make progress—that is, nodes will continue to propose, process, and decide on values without indefinitely stalling.

FLP Impossibility Theorem

Q: What does the FLP impossibility theorem state regarding consensus in asynchronous systems? How do the two definition of the FLP theorem relate?
A: It states that in an asynchronous system, you cannot guarantee both safety and liveness in the presence of network partitions.
The two definition are:

  1. In asynchronous distributed systems, consensus is impossible
  2. In asynchronous distributed systems, liveness and safety cannot be guaranteed at the same time
    The first definition (consensus is impossible) can be seen as a direct consequence of the second definition (liveness and safety cannot be guaranteed at the same time). If a consensus algorithm tries to ensure both safety and liveness, it may end up in a situation where, due to asynchrony, it cannot make progress (liveness fails) or may reach a consensus on an incorrect value (safety fails).

2PC vs. 3PC

Q: What is the main issue with Two-Phase Commit (2PC) that Three-Phase Commit (3PC) tries to solve?
A: 2PC can lead to blocking if the coordinator fails, whereas 3PC introduces a pre-commit phase to avoid blocking in synchronous systems.

Q: Why is 3PC more robust than 2PC?
A: 3PC introduces an additional phase that avoids a situation where participants can be blocked indefinitely if the coordinator fails.

Failure Scenarios in Commit Protocols

Q: What happens if a participant fails during the Two-Phase Commit (2PC) protocol?
A: If a participant fails during 2PC, the coordinator waits for a timeout. If no response is received, it assumes the participant has failed and aborts the transaction.

Q: What are the possible scenarios if a coordinator fails during the Two-Phase Commit (2PC) protocol?
A:

  • If a participant is in the Init state, the coordinator must have failed before completing the prepare phase, so the participant can abort the transaction.
  • If a participant is in the Ready state, it cannot decide on its own and may either wait for the coordinator to recover or ask other participants for their status, leading to blocking.

Q: How does the Three-Phase Commit (3PC) protocol handle participant failure?
A:

  • If a participant fails before voting (in the Init state), the coordinator assumes the participant will abort.
  • If the failure occurs in the Pre-commit phase, the coordinator can still commit and notify the failed participant to commit once it recovers.

Q: What is a blocking protocol, and why is 2PC considered one?
A: A blocking protocol is one where participants may become stuck in an indeterminate state, unable to reach a decision. 2PC is considered a blocking protocol because if the coordinator fails after participants have voted "yes" but before they receive a global decision, they may remain blocked indefinitely, unable to commit or abort.

Leader Election in Raft

Q: How does Raft ensure that split votes do not lead to indefinite elections?
A: Raft uses randomized timeouts to minimize the likelihood of two nodes becoming candidates at the same time, which reduces the chances of split votes during leader elections.

Q: What happens in Raft if no candidate wins a majority in an election?
A: The term ends prematurely, and a new election is started in the next term. Raft prevents indefinite terms with no leader by retrying the election process.

Paxos Detailed

Q: What is the key challenge with Paxos that makes it difficult to implement in practice?
A: The complexity of handling multiple rounds of proposals, rejections, and acceptance processes in an asynchronous environment, as well as managing the failure scenarios, makes Paxos difficult to understand and implement.

Q: How does Paxos guarantee consensus in the presence of failures?
A: Paxos ensures that even if some nodes fail, as long as a majority of nodes (quorum) can agree on a proposal, consensus is achieved. However, reaching this agreement can require multiple rounds of communication.

Comparing 2PC and 3PC

Q: What is the main advantage of 3PC over 2PC?
A: The main advantage of 3PC is that it introduces a Pre-commit phase, which helps to avoid blocking scenarios, making 3PC a non-blocking protocol in synchronous systems.

Q: What is the major downside of Three-Phase Commit (3PC) compared to Two-Phase Commit (2PC)?
A: 3PC is more expensive and complex, requiring more phases and messages to ensure that participants reach a decision, which makes it less practical in many real-world applications.

Fault Tolerance in Replicated State Machines

Q: What is the role of a replicated log in a fault-tolerant service?
A: The replicated log ensures that all replicas process the same sequence of operations in the same order, which is crucial for maintaining consistency across all replicas.

Q: How do replicated state machines achieve fault tolerance?
A: By replicating state across multiple servers and coordinating interactions between client requests and server replicas, replicated state machines ensure that failures of individual nodes do not affect the system’s ability to process commands consistently.

Safety vs. Liveness in Consensus

Q: What is the trade-off between safety and liveness in consensus protocols?
A: Safety guarantees that all non-failing nodes agree on the same order of operations, while liveness ensures that the system continues to make progress. Achieving both can be difficult, especially in the presence of failures or network partitions (as per the FLP theorem).

Q: How does the FLP theorem impact the design of consensus algorithms like Raft and Paxos?
A: The FLP theorem shows that in asynchronous systems, it is impossible to guarantee both safety and liveness simultaneously in the presence of network failures. Consensus algorithms must balance these two properties, often prioritizing safety at the expense of liveness during network partitions.

Failure Models in Consensus Protocols

Q: What failure assumptions are made in consensus protocols like Raft and Paxos?
A:

  1. Unreliable communication: Messages may be delayed, lost, or reordered.
  2. Crash failures: Processes may crash and later recover, but they must remember their previous state upon recovery.

Q: How do consensus protocols deal with node failures and recovery?
A: Consensus protocols assume that nodes may fail and recover, but nodes must retain information about their prior state (e.g., log entries, votes) to ensure that the system remains consistent after recovery.

General Guarantees in Consensus Protocols

Q: What are the safety guarantees in Raft and Paxos?
A: In both Raft and Paxos, safety ensures that all nodes that do not fail will execute the same commands in the same order, preventing divergent states among the nodes.

Q: What are the liveness guarantees in Raft and Paxos?
A: Liveness guarantees that the system continues to make progress as long as a majority of nodes are operational and can communicate with each other. However, liveness may not be guaranteed during network partitions or under certain failure conditions.

Synchronous vs. Asynchronous Systems

Q: Why are consensus algorithms like Raft more efficient in synchronous systems?
A: In synchronous systems, timeouts can be used to detect failures more reliably, making it easier to implement non-blocking protocols like 3PC or ensure timely leader elections in Raft.

Q: What challenges do asynchronous systems pose for consensus protocols in the context of Agreement?
A: In asynchronous systems, timeouts cannot reliably distinguish between slow nodes and failed nodes, making it harder to guarantee both safety and liveness, especially during network partitions.

Q: How do clients interact with the leader in Raft? How can they know who the current leader is when they back up after a fail?
A: In Raft, clients always interact with the leader. When a client starts, it connects to a random server, which then informs the client who the current leader is.

Q: What semantics does Raft guarantee for client operations?
A: Raft guarantees linearizable semantics, meaning all operations behave as if they were executed once and only once on a single copy.

Q: Where are Raft logs stored, and why?
A: Raft logs are stored on disk by servers to ensure they survive process failures.

Q: How does Raft handle log inconsistencies after a crash?
A: The leader assumes its log is correct, and normal operations will repair any inconsistencies.

Q: What is the log matching property in Raft?
A: The log matching property ensures that logs across all replicas remain consistent. It is based on two principles:

  1. Log entries with the same index and term store the same command.
  2. If a server has a log entry at a specific index term, all earlier entries in the log must match the leader's log.

Q: What does the log matching property ensure in Raft?
A: It ensures that if two logs have an entry at the same index and term, they store the same command, and all prior log entries are identical.

Q: How do followers check log consistency in Raft?
A: Followers check consistency by verifying the <index, term> pair of the previous log entry sent by the leader in an AppendEntries message. If the follower does not match, it rejects the request, and the leader retries with a lower index.

Q: How does Raft ensure log consistency when propagating log entries?
A: Raft uses the same AppendEntries message to both propagate log entries and find log inconsistencies.

Q: What is the leader completeness property in Raft?
A: Once a log entry has been committed, all future leaders must store that entry. Servers with incomplete logs must not get elected.

Q: How does a candidate prove its log is complete during leader election in Raft?
A: The candidate includes the index and term of its last log entry in the RequestVote message. Servers deny votes to candidates with less up-to-date logs.

Q: How are replicated state machines and commit protocols used in distributed DBMS?
A: Distributed DBMS often use two layers:

  1. First layer: The database is partitioned, and 2PC commit protocol is used for atomic commit across partitions.
  2. Second layer: Replicated state machines ensure multiple machines behave as one when managing partitions.

Q: What are the two layers in modern distributed DBMS?
A:

  1. The first layer handles database partitioning and ensures atomic commit across partitions, often using the 2PC commit protocol.
  2. The second layer uses replicated state machines to ensure multiple machines managing the partitions behave as one.

Q: How can state machine replication be extended for Byzantine processes?
A: State machine replication can be extended to handle byzantine processes, forming Byzantine Fault Tolerant (BFT) consensus.

Q: What consensus mechanism do cryptocurrencies rely on?
A: Cryptocurrencies can be viewed as replicated state machines that use distributed consensus on account balances, often relying on Blockchain technology.

Q: What is the relationship between cryptocurrencies and state machine replication?
A: Cryptocurrencies operate as replicated state machines where consensus on account balances is achieved through distributed mechanisms, often using blockchain.
