What is computer ethics? - Moor

Special technologies means special ethical issues.

A proposed definition

What is computer ethics
Computer ethics is the analysis of the ethical and social impact of computer technologies and the related formulation of new policies for the ethical use of such technologies.

Why new policies?
since computer technologies give us much more capabilities, the development of computer ethics is necessary for guiding us in deciding which action to pursue, through the formulation of policies.
For instance, in the case of policy for protecting computer programs we need to decide what is a computer program (idea, algorithm, machine-code. The concept of computer programs is new.

The conceptual vacuum
However, often there's not only a policy vacuum but a conceptual vacuum, so there's the problem of defining the conceptual framework within which the policies for action must be formulated.

However, it may be difficult to formulate an action policy even in a well-described conceptual framework. This happens because defining such policies requires considering different alternatives and so evaluating and thinking about our values.

For instance, assuming
software is intellectual property, why should intellectual property be protected?

Mark of a computer ethics problem
A computer ethics problem is a problem involving computer technologies and there is uncertainty about to do and even about how to understand the situation.

Computer ethics and science
Obviously to deal with computer ethics. It is necessary to deal with ethics but also with science behind computer technologies.

Conclusion
Computer ethics is a field of study which considers relationships among facts, conceptualizations, policies and values with regards to constantly changing technology.

The revolutionary machine

Why are computers revolutionary?
What is the computer characteristic that makes them revolutionary.
The fact they are very abundant and affordable is not enough (also pencils are).
The fact that they are new is not really true.
The fact that they are more powerful, small and user friendly than before is important but not not revolutionary characteristics (es: electric can openers have the same characteristics).
Logical malleability is the characteristic that makes computers revolutionary. The fact that computers can be shaped and modeled in order to do any activity is the hearth of the computer technologies revolution. Just as the power of the steam engine was the raw source at the center of the Industrial Revolution, the computer logic is at the center of the Computer Revolution. Potential limitless applications.

Logical malleability and policies
For the correct formulation of policies on the use of the computer is crucial to understand its main characteristic: logical malleability.

Syntactic and semantic malleability
Other conceptions of computers different from logical malleable tools might not be a good basis for the goal of formulating a policies. For example, the conception of computers as mere big calculators could lead to prioritizing scientific applications of computers, which restricts the potential field of action of computers.
Computers are both syntactically and semantically malleable: syntactical because of the infinite ways a computer can represent states and operations, semantically because those states can be taken to represent anything

Anatomy of the computer revolution

Computer revolution like Industrial Revolution
The computer revolution will likely evolve in a similar way as the industrial revolution:

  • first stage: introduction of the new technologies
  • second stage: technological permeation in the society
    The forces of industrialization dramatically transformed the society and so will the computer revolution.

Transformation
"transformed" means that the basic nature or purpose of an activity or institution is changed. We can see the transformation by focusing on the kinds of questions that are asked, typically:

  • in the first stage: "how well do the computer perform such an activity?"
  • In the second stage: "what is the nature and value of such and such an activity?", since computer become an integral part of the activity.

es:

  • "how efficiently do computers count votes in a fair election?" --> "what is a fair election?"
  • "how well do computers help us work?" --> "what is the nature of this work?"

With technology revolution, the concepts of activities begin to change, so the values associated with the old concept will have to be reexamined.

Computer ethics as practically important
These transformations will leave us with policy and conceptual vacuums about how to use computer technology, which means that computer ethics is a field of a substantial practical importance

The invisibility factor

Another argument for the importance of computer ethics for the ones who don't share the vision of the Computer Revolution lays in the invisibility factor

What is the invisibility factor
Computers most of the times perform operations in ways which are invisible to us. This means that there is an invisibility factor which can generate policy vacuums about how to use computer technology.

Three kinds of invisibility:

  • Invisible abuse
  • Invisible programming values
  • Invisible complex calculations

Invisible abuse
Computer can be used to perform abuse which are most of the times invisible. So the invisible abuse is the intentional use of invisible operations of a computer to engage in unethical conduct. Es: surveillance.

Invisible programming values
When a programmer codes a program, he follows the specification he is given, however there are many choices which are not specified and so on which is free to decide. This decision takes with them some implicit values which will then be embedded in the computer program. Even if one tries to be as unbiased as possible, implementation choices will build certain values into the program since the programmer makes some value judgement about what is important and what is not. This values become embedded in the final product and may be invisible to someone who runs the program.

Es: for an flight reservation service, deciding the airlines list order as alphabetic will advantage some flights companies or others.

Invisible complex calculations
Computers can perform calculations which are too complex for human inspection and understanding, so those calculations become invisible since it is impossible for humans to inspect them. Since it is impossible to do so, what should be our policy about trusting invisible calculations?

How to deal with invisibility factor?
The challenge for computer ethics is to formulate policies which will help us deal with the dilemma which sees exploiting computer efficiency and being vulnerable because of that efficiency. We must decide when to trust computer and when to trust them or not. This is another reason why computer ethics is so important.
