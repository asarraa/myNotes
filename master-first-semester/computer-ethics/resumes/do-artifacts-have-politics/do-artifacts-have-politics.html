Do Artifacts have politics? - Winner

Introduction

Considering artifacts as having political implications or values is not a new idea. In fact, critics of large-scale, high-technology systems argue that these systems often embody authoritarian values. Conversely, proponents who claim that science and technology enhance democracy, freedom, and social justice also acknowledge the political significance of artifacts.

However, this matter has been studied from to extreme points of view:

  • Social determinism of technology: technologies are just "tools", they don't carry any values, what matters is the social context in which they are embedded.
  • Technological determinism: technologies carry values themselves which are only result of internal dynamic, thus not influenced by the external influences, and society adapts to these values.

Theory of technological politics

A new theory is the theory of technological politics: it complements the social determinism by identifying certain technologies as political phenomena in their own right. It is based on:

  • How large-scale socio-technical systems evolve
  • How modern society respond to this evolution
  • How human ends adapt to those technological ends
    Thus, it offers a new social framework to interpret social patterns.
Politics

Politics are arrangements of power and authority in human associations as well as the activities that take place within those arrangements.

Artifacts, that is everything which is result of human activity, have embedded political values. We can divide technologies in two types:

Technical arrangements as form of order

Technologies often carry political and social implications, either deliberately or unintentionally. While they may appear neutral, they frequently serve as tools to express human motives and enforce specific social structures. For instance:

  • Overpasses in the USA were designed to prevent buses (and thus lower-income groups) from accessing certain areas, reflecting racist intentions.
  • Pneumatic molding machines had anti-union political significance.

Advantages and Disadvantages in Technological Implementation

Most technologies benefit certain groups while disadvantaging others, even when such effects are unintended. For example:

  • Architectural barriers exclude individuals with disabilities, forcing specific social structures without explicit intent.

By favoring some parties, technologies can create or reinforce social hierarchies. Moreover, the social context shapes the development of technologies, embedding specific values into their design. In the tomato harvester example, the machine radically transformed the agricultural sector, aligning with corporate profit motives, even though researchers did not intend this outcome. In fact, since the technology development and corporate profits are deeply connected in the ongoing social structure, it is this influence on the development of the machine that took to that outcome.
Here, the machine is not just symbolic of the political order—it embodies it.

Technologies as Social and Political Forces

Technologies are ways to order our world. The decisions made during the development process—down to small design features—carry profound social implications. These choices, whether intentional or not, embed values that directly impact society. Since technologies can be so powerful in ordering society, their development is similar to legislative acts, thus the issues that divide or unite people in society are settled not only in politics choices but also in technological devices.

The Role of Context in Technological Values

The political and social values embedded in technologies depend heavily on the context of their development:

  • The overpass example highlights how biases in design can enforce exclusion.
  • The critique of architectural barriers shows how raising awareness about design details can challenge and reshape the values technologies embed.

Inherently Political technologies

Technologies which for their very nature can be used only in certain kinds of political structure. Engels and Plato suggested that for certain kinds of complex systems (es: complex factory or a ship), it is necessary to be able to take firm decisions, thus there is a need of an authoritarian structure for using some kinds of technologies. That is, those artifacts carry in their very nature a political value. In contrary, Marks thought that the technology itself would allow to avoid those kind of hierarchies, and so it suggested that the political social structure and the types of technologies could be easily separated. (es: power plants: centralized, authoritarian vs solar panels: decentralized, democratic).
Even when a certain technological system seems to be strictly linked with a certain social organization, two possibilities can be true:

  • the system requires a certain social structure to work. Without that social structure the system cannot work since difficulties in its management will arise
  • the system doesn't require a certain social structure but it is better managed with a certain type of structure
    We seen that the major part of large scale technological systems are managed in a centralized and authoritarian social organization, but is it something that the system requires (or is managed better with) or is it just something that the ruling classes and institutions try to impose?
    To better understand if a large scale technological system can be managed in a different society structure, we should inspect the moral claims of practical necessity (e.g., "a ship must have a single captain") and weight them against moral claims of other sorts (e.g., "crew members have the right to participate in decision-making"). This process of weighing allows us to determine whether the practical reasons are sufficiently compelling to justify adopting the technology and its associated social structures—or whether alternative arrangements might be possible and desirable. So it is true that technological systems can carry embedded politics, however this doesn't mean that that those politics are fixed and force a certain social structure, but are something that can be compared to other social claims in order to find an organization structure that accommodates the politics embedded in the technologies while also taking into account other necessities

The problem is that usually moral claims which are not about practical reasons are treated like idealistic and this is especially true inside the context of technological system, where the moral reasons of the rest of society are almost no value when compared to practical and efficiency reasons. One example of comparison between practical moral claims and moral claims of different sort is the one about running a nuclear power plant: security of a power plant vs surveillance on populations (Privacy). Once moral claims have been compared, it is possible to understand if the practical reasons are morally important and actually require a certain society organization.
Furthermore, some technologies could require more attention and safety and that could bring to more surveillance on the population: other political implications embedded in those systems.
