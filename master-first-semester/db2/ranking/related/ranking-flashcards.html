Multi-objective Optimization

Q: What is the main problem of multi-objective optimization?
A: Given N objects described by d attributes and some notion of goodness, find the best k objects.

Rank Aggregation

Q: What is the problem of rank aggregation?
A: Rank aggregation is the problem of combining several ranked lists of objects into a single consensus ranking in a robust way.

Q: What are the two historical methods for rank aggregation?
A: The Borda method, which assigns penalties based on rank positions, and the Condorcet method, which selects the candidate that wins in all pairwise majority elections.

Q: What is a Condorcet winner?
A: A candidate who defeats every other candidate in pairwise majority rule elections. A Condorcet winner may not exist in the case of cyclic preferences.

Q: What does Arrow's Impossibility Theorem state in rank aggregation?
A: No rank-order electoral system can satisfy all fairness criteria (Non-dictatorship, Pareto Efficiency, Independence of Irrelevant Alternatives) simultaneously when there are three or more alternatives.

Q: What are the axioms required for a fair aggregation function in Arrowâs Theorem?
A: Non-dictatorship, Pareto Efficiency, and Independence of Irrelevant Alternatives (IIA).

Metric Approach to Rank Aggregation

Q: What is the metric approach to rank aggregation?
A: It consists of finding a new ranking R that minimizes the total distance to the initial rankings .

Q: What are two common metrics for comparing rankings?
A: Kendall tau distance, which counts the number of exchanges in a bubble sort, and Spearmanâs footrule, which measures the sum of rank differences between two lists.

Q: What is the relationship between Kendall tau distance and Spearman's footrule?
A: The inequality shows that Spearman's footrule can approximate Kendall tau distance.

Opaque Rankings

Q: What are opaque rankings?
A: Opaque rankings are techniques that only use the position of elements in the ranking to aggregate rankings, without using any associated score.

Q: Which algorithm is commonly used for opaque rankings?
A: The Median ranking algorithm.

Median Ranking Algorithm

Q: What is the problem solved by the median ranking algorithm?
A: Given n rankings of objects, aggregate these rankings into a single ranking that minimizes total disagreement with the individual rankings.

Q: What is the goal of the median ranking algorithm?
A: To determine the k top elements that appear in more than half of the ranked lists.

Q: How does the median ranking algorithm identify the top k elements?
A: By using sorted access in each list until there are k elements that occur in more than half of the lists.

Top-k Queries

Q: What is the aim of a top-k query?
A: To retrieve only the k best answers from a potentially large result set.

Q: What are the two essential operations for a top-k query in SQL?
A: Ordering the tuples by their scores (using ORDER BY) and limiting the result set to k tuples (using FETCH FIRST k ROWS ONLY).

Geometric View of Top-k Queries

Q: How can preferences be represented in a geometric view for top-k queries?
A: Each tuple can be represented in an n-dimensional space, where preferences are modeled as weights assigned to each attribute. The sum of attribute values multiplied by their weights defines the ranking function.

Q: How does the geometric view relate to top-k queries?
A: The preferences translate into straight lines or planes in the attribute space, and the goal is to find tuples that are closest to a target value in the ranking function.

Top-k 1-1 Join Queries

Q: What is a top-k 1-1 join query?
A: A query where all joins are on a common key attribute, and the goal is to find the top-k results based on the scoring function.

Q: What are the two main scenarios in top-k 1-1 join queries?
A: Indexed retrieval, where data is retrieved using indexes for efficient lookups, and the middleware scenario, where data is spread across multiple sources and combined by the middleware.

Q: What are the two access methods required for efficient top-k 1-1 join queries?
A: Sorted access, where objects are provided in rank order based on partial scores, and random access, where partial scores for specific objects are retrieved directly using their keys.

B0 Algorithm

Q: What is the B0 algorithm?
A: The B0 algorithm is a top-k algorithm where the scoring function is MAX, meaning it selects the top-k objects based on the highest individual score from any source.

Q: Who developed the B0 algorithm?
A: The B0 algorithm was developed by Ronald Fagin.

Q: What is the scoring function in the B0 algorithm?
A: The scoring function is the maximum value among the scores for each object from the different data sources.

K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN)

Q: What is the K-nearest neighbors (KNN) problem?
A: The KNN problem involves, given a point q, identifying the k tuples in a relation R that are closest to q according to a distance function.

Q: How does KNN differ from top-k queries?
A: In top-k queries, results are ranked by score, while in KNN, results are ranked by distance to a query point q.

Q: What is the goal in KNN queries?
A: To determine the k tuples in R that are closest to the query point q, based on a specified distance function.

Distance Functions for K-Nearest Neighbors

Q: What are some common distance functions used in KNN?
A: Euclidean distance, Manhattan distance, and Chebyshev distance.

Q: What is Euclidean distance?
A: Euclidean distance is the straight-line distance between two points in n-dimensional space, calculated as the square root of the sum of the squared differences between corresponding attributes.

Q: What is Manhattan distance?
A: Manhattan distance is the sum of the absolute differences between the coordinates of two points, often visualized as a path along grid lines.

Q: What is Chebyshev distance?
A: Chebyshev distance is the maximum absolute difference between the coordinates of two points in any dimension.

Impact of Weights on Distance Functions

Q: How do weights affect distance functions in KNN?
A: Weights affect the relative importance of each attribute when calculating distances, altering how "close" or "far" points are from the query point q.

Q: What is an iso-distance surface in the context of distance functions?
A: An iso-distance surface is a contour representing points that are equidistant from the query point q in attribute space.

Q: How does the shape of iso-distance surfaces differ among distance functions?
A: For Euclidean distance, iso-distance surfaces are spheres, for Manhattan distance they are diamond-shaped, and for Chebyshev distance, they are squares or cubes.

Top-k Queries with Normalized Scores

Q: How are scores normalized in top-k queries?
A: Scores from different attributes or sources are normalized to ensure that they are comparable, typically by scaling them to a common range.

Q: How is the global score of an object computed when scores are normalized?
A: The global score is computed by applying a scoring function (e.g., SUM, MAX, AVG) that combines the normalized scores of the object from different attributes or sources.

Score Space and Iso-score Curves

Q: What is a score space in the context of top-k queries?
A: Score space is an n-dimensional space where each dimension represents a ranking attribute, and each point represents a tupleâs score along those dimensions.

Q: What are iso-score curves?
A: Iso-score curves represent points in score space that have the same global score, similar to contours on a map that represent points of equal height.

Q: How are iso-score curves used in top-k queries?
A: They help identify objects with the same score and visualize how changes in weights or attributes affect ranking.

Common Scoring Functions

Q: What are some examples of common scoring functions in top-k queries?
A: Examples include SUM (adding up all scores), AVG (calculating the average score), and MAX (taking the highest score).

Q: How does the SUM scoring function work in top-k queries?
A: The SUM function adds the scores of an object across multiple attributes or sources to calculate its global score.

Q: How does the MAX scoring function differ from SUM in top-k queries?
A: MAX selects the highest score among the object's attributes or sources, while SUM adds up all scores.

Equally Scored Objects in Top-k Queries

Q: What are equally scored objects in top-k queries?
A: Equally scored objects are those that have the same global score, typically found by looking at iso-score curves in score space.

Q: How can equally scored objects impact ranking in top-k queries?
A: If multiple objects have the same score, they may be ranked equally or tie in the final ranking, affecting the selection of top-k results.

Instance Optimality and Top-k Queries

Q: What does instance optimality mean in the context of top-k algorithms?
A: Instance optimality means that the algorithm performs as well as the best possible algorithm for each specific instance, given the constraints.

Q: What is the instance optimality ratio for the median ranking algorithm?
A: The optimality ratio is 2 with an additive constant of m, and it can be reduced to 4 when removing dependence on m.

Ranked Lists in Top-k Queries

Q: What is the role of ranked lists in top-k queries?
A: Ranked lists represent partial rankings of objects from different sources, which are combined to find the overall top-k objects.

Q: How is random access used in ranked lists for top-k queries?
A: Random access allows direct retrieval of an objectâs partial score from any data source using its unique identifier.

Other topics

Q: What is Fagin's Algorithm (FA)?
A: FA is a top-k algorithm that retrieves the k best elements according to a monotone scoring function from different ranks using sorted and random accesses, but it does not exploit the scoring function.

Q: What is the Threshold Algorithm (TA)?
A: TA is a top-k algorithm that retrieves the k best elements according to a monotone scoring function from different ranks using sorted and random accesses, and it exploits the scoring function.

Q: What is the No Random Access algorithm (NRA)?
A: NRA is a top-k algorithm that retrieves the k best elements according to a monotone scoring function from different ranks without using random accesses.

Q: What are the pros of ranking queries?
A:

  1. Effective at identifying the best objects with respect to a scoring function.
  2. Provides excellent control of result cardinality (k).
  3. Efficient computation, potentially achieving instance optimality in some settings.
  4. Simplifies the expression of the relative importance of attributes.

Q: What are the cons of ranking queries?
A: The main con is the difficulty in specifying an appropriate scoring function for users.

Q: What is the problem Fagin's Algorithm (FA) tries to solve?
A: Given an integer k â¥ 1 and m ranked lists, the goal is to identify and return the top objects with the highest overall scores across the lists using random and sorted accesses.

Q: What are the key inputs and outputs of Fagin's Algorithm?
A:

  • Input: integer k, a monotone scoring function S, and m ranked lists (R_i).
  • Output: the top k <object, score> pairs according to S.

Q: What is the threshold in Fagin's Algorithm?
A: The threshold (T = S(s{L1}, ..., s{Lm})) is the score of the last seen object at a certain point, representing the worst among those seen but better than the next accessible values.

Q: Why does Fagin's Algorithm work?
A: It works by defining a threshold point, which is the smallest score among seen values. As the threshold score lowers, unseen points cannot surpass fully seen points until the algorithm identifies k best objects.

Q: What is the complexity of Fagin's Algorithm?
A: The complexity is sub-linear in the number of N elements, but the algorithm is not instance-optimal and has to buffer all objects accessed through sorted access, which may require significant memory.

Q: What is the problem the Threshold Algorithm (TA) solves?
A: Given an integer k â¥ 1 and m ranked lists, TA identifies the top objects with the highest overall scores across the lists by using random and sorted accesses and exploiting the scoring function.

Q: What are the inputs and outputs of the Threshold Algorithm (TA)?
A: the following

  • Input: integer k, a monotone scoring function S, and m ranked lists (R_i).
  • Output: the top k <object, score> pairs according to S.

Q: Why does the Threshold Algorithm work?
A: TA works by computing a threshold point during every row descent, creating a score line that divides points into regions of higher and lower scores. The algorithm stops when k elements are in the higher-score region.

Q: What is instance optimality in the context of TA?
A: TA is instance-optimal among algorithms using random and sorted accesses, meaning it performs as well as any other such algorithm in a given instance.

Q: How is middleware cost calculated for TA?
A: Middleware cost is calculated as:
[ cost = #SortedAccess \times Cost{sa} + #RandomAccess \times Cost{ra} ]
where (Cost{sa}) and (Cost{ra}) represent the respective costs of sorted and random accesses.

Q: What happens if random access is impossible in TA?
A: When random access is impossible, alternative algorithms like the B0 algorithm or NRA (No Random Access algorithm) are used, with the limitation that B0 only works if the scoring function S = MAX.

Q: What is the problem the No Random Access Algorithm (NRA) solves?
A: Given an integer k â¥ 1 and m ranked lists, NRA retrieves the top objects with the highest scores across the lists, but without using random accesses.

Q: What is the key idea behind the No Random Access Algorithm (NRA)?
A: NRA maintains lower and upper bounds on the score of each object retrieved by sorted access to estimate the object's final score without using random access.

Q: What is the optimality of the No Random Access Algorithm (NRA)?
A: NRA is instance-optimal with an optimal ratio of m, and the cost does not grow monotonically with k.

Skyline queries

Q: What is the goal of Skyline queries?
A: The goal of Skyline queries is to find good objects according to several different perspectives, based on the notion of dominance.

Q: When does tuple ( t ) dominate tuple ( s )?
A: Tuple ( t ) dominates tuple ( s ) if:

  1. ( t ) is nowhere worse than ( s ), and
  2. ( t ) is better than ( s ) at least once.

Q: How are lower values treated in Skyline queries compared to Ranking queries?
A: Lower values are typically considered better in Skyline queries, which is the opposite convention compared to Ranking queries.

Q: What is the Skyline of a relation?
A: The Skyline of a relation is the set of non-dominated tuples. A tuple is in the Skyline if it is the top-1 result with respect to at least one monotone scoring function, representing potentially optimal tuples.

Q: How is a Skyline different from a top-k query?
A: Skyline queries differ from top-k queries because there is no scoring function that always yields Skyline points in the first k positions across all possible instances.

Q: Is Skyline a standard feature in SQL?
A: No, Skyline is not part of the SQL standard, but a proposed syntax for Skyline queries exists and can be translated into SQL by performing dominance checks between tuples, as described in the BNL algorithm.

Q: What is the proposed SQL syntax for Skyline queries?
A: The proposed syntax for Skyline queries is:

SELECT ... FROM ... WHERE ...  
GROUP BY ... HAVING ...  
SKYLINE OF [DISTINCT] d1 [MIN | MAX | DIFF],  
                ..., dm [MIN | MAX | DIFF]  
ORDER BY ...

Q: What is the Block Nested Loop (BNL) algorithm for Skyline queries?
A: The BNL algorithm retrieves the Skyline by performing dominance checks between pairs of tuples in the dataset.

Q: What is the Sort Filter Skyline (SFS) algorithm?
A: The SFS algorithm retrieves the Skyline by performing dominance checks between pairs of tuples after pre-sorting the tuples by a monotone function.

Q: What are the pros of Skyline queries?
A: the following

  1. Effective at identifying potentially interesting objects when user preferences are unknown.
  2. Very simple to use, as no parameters are required.

Q: What are the cons of Skyline queries?
A: the following

  1. Not efficient due to quadratic complexity.
  2. Agnostic to known user preferences.
  3. Poor control over the result cardinality, especially in anti-correlated datasets.

Q: What is the K-Skyband extension of Skyline queries?
A: The K-Skyband approach retrieves tuples that are dominated by fewer than ( k ) other tuples. The classic Skyline is a K-Skyband with ( k = 1 ), retrieving tuples dominated by none.

Q: What is the problem solved by the Block Nested Loop (BNL) algorithm?
A: The BNL algorithm solves the problem of retrieving the Skyline from a dataset of multi-dimensional points by performing dominance checks.

Q: What are the inputs and outputs of the Block Nested Loop (BNL) algorithm?
A: the following

  • Input: A dataset ( D ) of multi-dimensional points.
  • Output: The Skyline of the dataset.

Q: What is the time complexity of the Block Nested Loop (BNL) algorithm?
A: The time complexity of the BNL algorithm is ( O(n^2) ), which is inefficient for large datasets in database contexts.

Q: What is the problem solved by the Sort Filter Skyline (SFS) algorithm?
A: The SFS algorithm solves the problem of retrieving the Skyline from a dataset of multi-dimensional points by pre-sorting and performing dominance checks.

Q: What are the inputs and outputs of the Sort Filter Skyline (SFS) algorithm?
A: the following

  • Input: A dataset ( D ) of multi-dimensional points.
  • Output: The Skyline of the dataset.

Q: What is the time complexity of the Sort Filter Skyline (SFS) algorithm?
A: The time complexity of the SFS algorithm is ( O(n^2) ), similar to BNL, making it inefficient for large datasets.
