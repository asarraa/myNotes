Q: [09 - 3] How does reinforcement learning differ from supervised learning?
A: In supervised learning, we train a model by providing it with the correct output, while in reinforcement learning, we train a model by providing it with an evaluation of its output. In reinforcement learning, there is no idea of right and wrong, just feedback on the quality of actions.

Q: [09 - 4] What is the agent-environment interface in reinforcement learning?
A: The agent-environment interface is a framework where an agent interacts with an environment by taking actions, observing states, and receiving rewards. The agent makes decisions while the environment responds to these actions, providing new states and rewards.

Q: [09 - 5] What are the key characteristics of sequential decision making?
A: In sequential decision making: 1) we take a sequence of decisions (or actions) to reach a goal, 2) optimal actions are context-dependent, 3) we generally do not have examples of correct actions, 4) actions may have long-term consequences, and 5) short-term consequences of optimal actions might seem negative.

A: The main components are: the set of states , the set of actions , and the reward function . The agent observes the state, takes an action, and receives a reward from the environment.

Q: [09 - 26] What is the Reward Hypothesis in reinforcement learning?
A: The Reward Hypothesis states that all goals and purposes can be well thought of as maximization of the expected value of the cumulative sum of a received scalar signal (reward).

Q: [09 - 26] What are two examples of reward design approaches?
A: Two examples are: 1) goal-reward representation: 1 for goal, 0 otherwise, and 2) action-penalty representation: -1 for not reaching the goal, 0 once goal is reached.

Q: [09 - 28] What is a policy in reinforcement learning and what types of policies exist?
A: A policy is what decides which action the agent selects at any given point and fully defines the behavior of an agent. Policies can be: Markovian/Non-Markovian, Deterministic/Stochastic, and Stationary/Non-Stationary.

Q: [09 - 29] How is a deterministic policy defined mathematically?
A: A deterministic policy is defined as a function that maps each state to a specific action.
