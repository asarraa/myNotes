Q: [10-6] What is Dynamic Programming (DP) and why is it needed for solving MDPs?
A: Dynamic Programming is a method that solves complex problems by breaking them down into simpler sub-problems in a recursive manner. It's needed for solving Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) because a brute-force approach would be computationally infeasible, requiring evaluation of deterministic policies and solving linear equations for each policy.

Q: [10-16] What is Policy Iteration and what are its two main steps?
A: Policy Iteration is an algorithm to find the optimal policy by alternating between two steps: Policy Evaluation (computing the value function of the current policy) and Policy Improvement (making the policy greedy with respect to its value function).

Q: [10-17] How does Policy Iteration exploit the Policy Improvement Theorem?
A: Policy Iteration exploits the Policy Improvement Theorem by repeatedly evaluating a policy and then improving it by acting greedily with respect to its value function. This process continues until the policy stabilizes, at which point it must be the optimal policy.

Q: [10-18] How do we determine if a policy is stable in the Policy Iteration algorithm?
A: A policy is considered stable if the policy improvement step doesn't change the selected action in any state. If the policy changes in any state, we note that the policy is still changing and set "policy stable" to false, then continue with another iteration of evaluation and improvement.

Q: [10-17] What is the stopping condition for the Policy Iteration algorithm?
A: The Policy Iteration algorithm stops when the policy becomes stable, meaning that the policy improvement step doesn't change the action selected in any state. At this point, the current policy must be optimal.

Q: [10 - 33] What is Generalized Policy Iteration?
A: Generalized Policy Iteration (GPI) is a framework that allows finding the optimal policy by interleaving partial evaluation and improvement steps, rather than requiring complete policy evaluation before improvement. It alternates between policy evaluation and policy improvement until convergence.

Q: [10 - 33] How does Policy Iteration differ from Generalized Policy Iteration?
A: Policy Iteration alternates complete policy evaluation and improvement up to convergence, while Generalized Policy Iteration allows for finding the optimal policy by interleaving partial evaluation and improvement steps.

Q: [10 - 34] What is Value Iteration?
A: Value Iteration is a popular Generalized Policy Iteration method where only a single sweep of updates is performed in the policy evaluation step, and then the value function is immediately updated using the Bellman optimality equation.

Q: [10 - 36] What are the two major issues with Dynamic Programming techniques?
A: The two major issues with Dynamic Programming are: 1) They require complete knowledge of the MDP (one-step dynamics), and 2) They face computational limitations, being applicable only to problems with a few million states.

Q: [10 - 37] What is Asynchronous Dynamic Programming?
A: Asynchronous Dynamic Programming is a method that does not use exhaustive sweeps of the entire state set. Instead, it picks states at random, applies the appropriate backup, and repeats until a convergence criterion is met.

Q: [10 - 37] How can states be selected more intelligently in Asynchronous Dynamic Programming?
A: States can be selected more intelligently using an agent's experience as a guide, rather than selecting them randomly.

Q: [10 - 38] What is the "curse of dimensionality" in the context of Dynamic Programming?
A: The "curse of dimensionality" refers to the problem that the number of states often grows exponentially with the number of state variables, making classical DP impractical for large problems.
