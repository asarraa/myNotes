Performance Modeling in Computing Infrastructures

Introduction to Performance Modeling

Performance is a critical aspect of computing systems that focuses on the total effectiveness of computer systems, encompassing throughput, individual response time, and availability. Essentially, performance characterizes the amount of useful work accomplished by a computer system compared to the time and resources used. In today's competitive technological landscape, performance has become increasingly important due to its direct impact on business success and user satisfaction.

The significance of performance can be illustrated by real-world examples: In 2006, Amazon discovered that every additional 100ms of latency in page load time resulted in a 1% decrease in salesâa loss that would today equate to approximately $3.8 billion. Similarly, Google found that an additional 0.5 seconds in search page generation time led to a 20% drop in traffic. In financial sectors, brokers could lose up to $4 million in revenue per millisecond if their trading platform is 5 milliseconds slower than competitors. These examples underscore why performance modeling and optimization are crucial aspects of computing infrastructure design and management.

System Quality Assessment Approaches

When evaluating system quality, particularly performance, there are several approaches available:

  • Intuitive approaches rely on experience and trend extrapolation. While these methods are rapid and flexible, they often lack accuracy and depend heavily on the rare individuals who possess sufficient intuitive skills in this domain.

  • Experimental evaluation involves directly testing alternatives. This approach provides excellent accuracy but is often prohibitively expensive, laborious, and inflexible. Additionally, experiments typically yield accurate knowledge about system behavior under one specific set of assumptions, without providing insight that would allow generalizing to other scenarios.

  • Model-based approaches offer a balanced solution. Since systems are complex, creating an abstraction (model) helps distill the essential aspects of system behavior while ignoring unnecessary details. These models can drive design decisions, such as architecture selection or resource allocation to meet performance goals. Often, especially during the design phase, models may be the only artifacts available to work with before the actual system exists.

Quality Evaluation Techniques

Quality evaluation techniques can be broadly categorized into two main approaches: measurement-based and model-based methods.

Measurement-based techniques include direct measurement, benchmarking, and prototyping. These approaches provide concrete data from real or simulated systems but may require significant resources and existing infrastructure.

Model-based techniques include analytical and numerical models, simulation, and hybrid techniques. Analytical and numerical techniques apply mathematical methods, often leveraging probability theory and stochastic processes. While these are the most efficient and precise approaches, they're only available in limited cases where the mathematical foundations have been established. Simulation techniques reproduce traces of system behavior through software modeling and are more general but potentially less accurate, especially for rare events. Hybrid techniques combine analytical/numerical methods with simulation to leverage the strengths of both approaches.

Queueing Networks: The Reference Model

Queueing theory forms the foundation for performance modeling in this course. At its core, queueing theory addresses what happens when numerous jobs compete for scarce resources, resulting in queues and delays. This scenario is prevalent throughout computing systems:

  • CPUs use time-sharing schedulers to manage multiple processes
  • Disks serve queues of read/write requests
  • Network routers process queues of packets waiting to be routed
  • Databases manage lock queues where transactions wait to acquire locks on records

A queueing network is a collection of service centers representing system resources and customers representing users or transactions. The power of queueing network modeling lies in its ability to abstract away low-level system details while capturing high-level performance characteristics.

Service Center Components

A service center consists of several key components that work together to process incoming requests:

Arrivals

Arrivals represent jobs entering the system and specify how fast, how often, and which types of jobs the station services. The primary metric of interest is the average arrival rate Î» (requests per second). Arrivals can come from external sources, from other queues within the network, or even from the same queue through loop-back connections.

Service and Service Time

The service component represents the time a job spends being processed by a server. The service time is characterized by its average duration, often expressed as 1/Î¼, where Î¼ is the maximum service rate. Service centers can have different configurations of servers:

  • Single server facilities can only serve one customer at a time, with waiting customers remaining in a buffer until chosen
  • Infinite server facilities have at least as many servers as customers, so each customer can be served immediately without queueing
  • Multiple server facilities have a fixed number (c) of servers, each capable of serving a customer simultaneously. If the number of customers exceeds c, additional customers must wait in the buffer

Queue (Buffer)

When jobs exceed the processing capacity of the system, they must wait in a queue or buffer. The buffer capacity can be finite or effectively infinite. With finite buffers, when capacity is reached, either:

  • New arrivals are suspended until capacity becomes available, or
  • New arrivals are lost (turned away) until capacity is available again

When a job completes service and leaves the system, one of the jobs in the queue can enter the now-free service center according to the service discipline.

Service Discipline (Queueing Policy)

The service discipline determines which waiting customer will next receive service when a server becomes available. Common service disciplines include:

  • FCFS (First-Come-First-Serve) or FIFO (First-In-First-Out): serves customers in order of arrival
  • LCFS (Last-Come-First-Serve) or LIFO (Last-In-First-Out): serves the most recently arrived customer first
  • RSS (Random-Selection-for-Service): selects the next customer randomly
  • PRI (Priority): assigns different priorities to elements of a population

Population

The population refers to the customers who might require service. Population characteristics can be:

  • Finite: with a fixed maximum number N of customers, where no more than N will ever require service simultaneously
  • Infinite: where the population is so large that there's no perceptible impact on the arrival process

Population members can be divided into classes when they're not all identical. Different classes may vary in characteristics such as arrival rate or service time, which is part of workload characterization.

Routing in Queueing Networks

In queueing networks, routing defines how jobs move between service centers after completing service at one center. Various routing algorithms determine the path a job will take:

  • Probabilistic routing: Each possible path has an assigned probability of being chosen
  • Round-robin routing: The destination rotates among all possible exits
  • Join the shortest queue: Jobs query the queue lengths of possible destinations and choose the one with the smallest waiting line

Types of Queueing Networks

Queueing networks can be classified into several types:

  • Open networks: Customers may arrive from or depart to some external environment
  • Closed networks: A fixed population of customers remain within the system, continuously circulating
  • Mixed networks: Contain classes of customers exhibiting both open and closed patterns of behavior

Examples and Applications of Queueing Networks

Open Network Example: Three-Tier Web Application

Consider a client-server system dealing with external arrivals, which is architected with three tiers: a web server, an application server, and a database server. We can model this system in two scenarios:

  1. When we only know that each server should be visited by the application, we can model it as a simple open network with sequential flows.
  2. When we know the detailed flow patterns (e.g., after visiting the web server, requests go to the application server and then either return to the web server and leave or go to the database and then back to the application server), we can model these specific routing probabilities.

Another example of an open network is a tandem queueing network, commonly used to model production lines where raw parts enter the system, go through several processing stages, and the final product leaves.

Closed Network Example: Internal Enterprise System

For a client-server system with a finite number of customers (e.g., employees within an organization), we can use a closed queueing network. If the system has the same three-tier architecture as above (web server, application server, database server), we need to include a representation of the "think time" of users between requests.

Level of Detail in Modeling

When creating queueing network models, the level of detail is an important consideration. It's generally best to proceed top-down, starting with a high-level model and adding detail only as needed. Since parameterizing the model (determining accurate values for service times, routing probabilities, etc.) is challenging, it's beneficial to keep the complexity low.

For example, a web server could be modeled at different levels of detail:

  • As a single service center
  • Breaking it down into CPU and disk components
  • Further detailing it with CPU, caches, RAM, and multiple disks

Practical Example: Wireless Access Gateway

To illustrate the application of queueing theory, consider a wireless access gateway:

  • Packets arrive at a mean rate of 125 packets per second and are buffered
  • The gateway takes 2 milliseconds on average to transmit a packet
  • The buffer has 13 places, including the place occupied by the packet being transmitted
  • Packets that arrive when the buffer is full are lost

This can be modeled as a single queue center with:

  • Finite queue capacity = 13
  • FCFS service discipline
  • Arrival rate Î» = 125 requests/second
  • Service rate Î¼ = 1/(2ms) = 500 requests/second

Using this model, we can determine if the buffer capacity is sufficient to ensure that fewer than one packet per million gets lost.

Conclusion

Performance modeling, particularly using queueing networks, provides a powerful framework for understanding and optimizing computing infrastructures. By abstracting complex systems into networks of service centers and customers, we can make predictions about system behavior, identify bottlenecks, and make informed design decisions.

As computing infrastructures continue to evolve in complexity and scale, from data centers to edge computing and IoT devices, the ability to model and predict performance becomes increasingly valuable. Queueing network theory, with its balance of mathematical rigor and practical applicability, offers a robust approach for tackling these challenges.

Whether you're designing a new system architecture, optimizing an existing infrastructure, or planning for future capacity needs, performance modeling provides the analytical tools needed to ensure that systems meet their performance requirements in a cost-effective manner.
